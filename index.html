Gender Career Inequality

Noah David Frassrand, Jingyan Li, Melody Yunhan Zhang



Introduction

It has been observed that women consistently earn lower than men, across occupations, across education levels, across countries. The Wage Gap is defined as systemic and institutionalized differences in earnings between men and women. In 2020, women earned 84% of what men earned, according to a Pew Research Center analysis of median hourly earnings of both full- and part-time workers. Based on this estimate, it would take an extra 42 days of work for women to earn what men did in 2020.

This effect is exacerbated in especially women of color. This is closely linked to the history of labor in America. Discrimination has historically been ingrained in the U.S., from depriving wages to Black women or stealing land and opportunity from Native women. There are lasting consequences to this history of oppression.

At the current rate of progress, the gender pay gap will not close until 2111. So why does the gender gap still persist? Much of the gap is driven by factors such as educational attainment, occupational segregation and work experience. Women as a whole are over-represented in lower-paying occupations, and underrepresented in high-paying ones, relative to their share of the workforce. While discrimination on the basis of sex has been prohibited, it still plays a role in the wage discrepancies.

Below, we will explore these disparities in income nationally and globally in order to better visualize patterns in gender wage gaps.

Women University Presidents on U.S. accredited, degree-granting institutions.

The graph below shows the change in percentage of university presidents who are women from 1986 to 2016. In 2016, 30.1% of university presidents were women, which is triple the percentage from 1986. Frances Elizabeth Willard became the first female college president in 1871, heading the Evanston College for Ladies in Illinois, which later merged with Northwestern University. In 1975, Lorene L. Rogers was the first woman to lead a major research university (University of Texas), and Judith Rodin in 1994 became the first permanent female president of an Ivy League institution (University of Pennsylvania). Despite these major accomplishments, there is still an uneven distribution of female presidents in American universities. Why are there so few women leading universities? A number of variables account for this lack of women in leadership roles, including lack of diversity in hiring practices, bias, and family life.

Conclusion

The gender wage gap is a persistent and systemic issue that affects women of all demographics and occupations. Efforts to close the wage gap must address the varying drivers of it as well as the multitude of biases that hold women—particularly women of color, LGBTQ women, and women with other diverse identities—and their families back. Looking forward, we must invest in equal pay reform to combat discriminatory practices in the workplace. For example, access to paid sick days and a comprehensive paid family and medical leave program are essential policies that would help minimize job loss and ensure better economic security for all workers. Beyond public policy, society must confront cultural biases that continue to harm women by devaluing their work and confining them to specific gender roles. This is an issue of economic security and equality—and women and their families cannot afford to wait for either.
