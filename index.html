Gender-based Inequalities in the Workplace

Noah David Frassrand, Jingyan Li, Melody Yunhan Zhang



Introduction

It has been observed that women consistently earn lower than men, across occupations, across education levels, across countries. The Wage Gap is defined as systemic and institutionalized differences in earnings between men and women. In 2020, women earned 84% of what men earned, according to a Pew Research Center analysis of median hourly earnings of both full- and part-time workers. Based on this estimate, it would take an extra 42 days of work for women to earn what men did in 2020.

This effect is especially exacerbated in women of color. This is closely linked to the history of labor in America. Discrimination has historically been ingrained in the U.S., from depriving wages to Black women or stealing land and opportunity from Native women. There are lasting consequences to this history of oppression.

At the current rate of progress, the gender pay gap will not close until 2111. So why does the gender gap still persist? Much of the gap is driven by factors such as educational attainment, occupational segregation and work experience. Women as a whole are over-represented in lower-paying occupations, and underrepresented in high-paying ones, relative to their share of the workforce. While discrimination on the basis of sex has been prohibited, it still plays a role in the wage discrepancies.

Below, we will explore these disparities in income nationally and globally in order to better visualize patterns in gender wage gaps.

Difference in median weekly earnings

US Bureau of Labor Statistics released weekly earnings of full-time wage and salary workers by sex, last modified in 2021. The graph below depicts the constant dollar trends of earnings over time. While both genders have experienced growth in wages over time, the gap between male and female salaries persists from 2012 to 2021. This effect has been driven by historical factors including occupational segregation, gender based discrimination, and access to medical and family leave.

Income Inequality By Occupations and Gender for all working American citizens as of January 2015.

This graph shows the weekly wage difference between men and women by occupation. It's important to note that regardless of occupation, there is always a gap. This is true even for occupations that are predominantly done by women. If we ignore age differences, experience, etc., and only compare men and women with the same jobs and qualifications, women make 98% of what men make. This is a brilliant illustration of how numerous factors are currently contributing to the wage gap, such as the fact that fewer women major in STEM fields. These differences often come from different stereotypes and pressures that are placed on women vs. men.

Income Inequality By Education and Gender

This graph depicts the income gap based on different educational backgrounds between men and women. On average, women are more likely to have a bachelor's degree among full-time workers, with 41.7% of women having one and 36.2% of men. With 18 million women obtaining this type of degree and 21 million men, this suggests that it is easier for men to get jobs that may be a stretch based on their qualifications, potentially contributing further to income inequality. Additionally, working women who haven't attained a college education tend to be older than their male counterparts, and for those who have attained a bachelor's, the men tend to be younger. These trends mean that the wage gap is slightly smaller among those without a bachelor's degree, since women tend to have more years of experience. However, the gap is still present.

Exploring the share of Fortune 500 companies with female chief executive officers

This graph depicts the number of women who are CEOs in the Fortune 500. In the past 25 years, the number of women CEO's has been steadily increasing, with the highest number being 6.4% in 2017, at least in our data set. Currently, there are 41 women CEOs, accounting for 8.2%. Some of this change has been from new companies making the rankings, while several have been from companies falling off the list due to decreasing revenues. Amidst this issue some companies, such as Nasdaq, have been looking into diversity mandates to encourage women and minorities to have higher positions in these companies. Some CEOs, like the CEO of Xerox from 2009-2016 Usrula Burns, originally opposed these mandates, but have mentioned that they might support them if companies don't start to change on their own.

Female University Presidents on U.S. accredited, degree-granting institutions.

The graph below shows the change in percentage of university presidents who are women from 1986 to 2016. In 2016, 30.1% of university presidents were women, which is triple the percentage from 1986. Frances Elizabeth Willard became the first female college president in 1871, heading the Evanston College for Ladies in Illinois, which later merged with Northwestern University. In 1975, Lorene L. Rogers was the first woman to lead a major research university (University of Texas), and Judith Rodin in 1994 became the first permanent female president of an Ivy League institution (University of Pennsylvania). Despite these major accomplishments, there is still an uneven distribution of female presidents in American universities. Why are there so few women leading universities? A number of variables account for this lack of women in leadership roles, including lack of diversity in hiring practices, bias, and family life.

Conclusion

The gender wage gap is a persistent and systemic issue that affects women of all demographics and occupations. Efforts to close the wage gap must address the varying drivers of it as well as the multitude of biases that hold women—particularly women of color, LGBTQ women, and women with other diverse identities—and their families back. Looking forward, we must invest in equal pay reform to combat discriminatory practices in the workplace. For example, access to paid sick days and a comprehensive paid family and medical leave program are essential policies that would help minimize job loss and ensure better economic security for all workers. Beyond public policy, society must confront cultural biases that continue to harm women by devaluing their work and confining them to specific gender roles. This is an issue of economic security and equality—and women and their families cannot afford to wait for either.
